# Layer 6 policy-evidence notes (working notes)

**Provenance:** working notes from the original policy-document review (2026-07). Every
quotation and printed-page reference below was accepted in that review but
**must be re-verified against the source PDFs before it is published in the Layer 6 matrix.**
Nothing here is a build input; this file preserves the detailed findings.

## What the prior review established

### 1. Scale-up is an explicit current policy instrument — RE-VERIFIED 2026-07-13

MSME Policy Note 2025–26, printed p.37 (PDF p.40), "Additional Capital Subsidy for Scaling Up":

> "All existing Micro and Small Manufacturing Enterprises, when they scale to higher category
> (from Micro to Small/Medium or from Small to Medium) by undertaking expansion /
> diversification."

Benefit: "5% Additional capital subsidy, maximum Rs. 25 lakh."

Correction to the recovered summary: eligibility covers micro→small **or medium** and
small→medium, and the benefit is a 5% additional capital subsidy capped at ₹25 lakh.
Establishment graduation across MSME categories is an active state instrument, not an
analytical idea invented by this project.

### 2. Hiring past 20 employees is subsidized — RE-VERIFIED 2026-07-13

MSME Policy Note 2025–26, printed p.38 (PDF p.40), section 2.3.2 payroll subsidy:

> "The scheme envisages reimbursement of employer's contribution to EPF for the first three
> years if the unit employs more than 20 persons subject to a maximum of Rs. 24,000 per
> employee per annum for the following enterprises."

Eligibility wording is "more than 20 persons" (strictly greater than 20, not 20-or-more).

Directly policy-relevant measured outcomes: employment structure around the 20-person level
and movement into EPF-covered (specified social-security) employment. EPF reimbursement does
not establish that written contracts or paid leave are explicit government priorities.

### 3. Government reports programme reach, not impact — RE-VERIFIED 2026-07-13

MSME Policy Note 2025–26, printed p.38 (PDF p.40):

> "During 2024-25, a subsidy of Rs.359.99 Crore has been disbursed to 2,481 enterprises."

**Attribution correction:** the sentence closes section 2.3.1 "Capital Subsidy" — it reports
capital-subsidy disbursement, not payroll-subsidy or scale-up-specific disbursement. The
matrix must not attribute this figure to the payroll subsidy.

This is an output metric (money disbursed, recipient count). It does not reveal recipient
growth, additional employment, counterfactual behaviour, job quality of supported jobs, or
displacement of non-recipients.

### 4. Industrial Policy 2021 targets are verified but historical — RE-VERIFIED 2026-07-13

Tamil Nadu Industrial Policy 2021, printed p.4 (PDF p.9), exact target wording:

- "Achieve annual growth rate of 15% in the manufacturing sector during the policy term."
- "Attract investments worth INR 10 lakh cr. (US$ 135 billion) between 2020 and 2025."
- "Create employment opportunities for 20 lakh (2 million) people by 2025."
- "Increase the contribution of the Manufacturing Sector to 30% of GSVA by 2030"

**Page correction:** the validity statement is on printed p.3 (PDF p.8), not p.7:

> "This Policy supersedes the Tamil Nadu Industrial Policy 2014 and will be valid till
> 31.03.2025."

These are verified historical priorities, not automatically current targets. Successor check:
see section 6 below.

### 5. Current budget evidence

The 2025–26 Budget Speech shows investment, MSME lending, industrial parks and job creation
remain active concerns — e.g. it announces the Tamil Nadu Semiconductor Mission–2030 with a
five-year allocation of ₹500 crore (p.49). It does not, by itself, renew the expired 2021
Industrial Policy targets.

### 6. Industrial Policy 2021 successor check — RESOLVED 2026-07-13: intent only

Web verification (July 2026) found **no enacted successor policy, no extension order, and no
notified new general Industrial Policy**. Specifically:

- The 2021 policy's own text: "This Policy supersedes the Tamil Nadu Industrial Policy 2014
  and will be valid till 31.03.2025" (printed p.3). No amendment found on official mirrors.
- investingintamilnadu.com still lists the 2021 policy unchanged; under this project's rules a
  portal listing is not evidence the policy remains in force.
- Newer documents are narrow sectoral policies, not general-policy successors: Circular
  Economy Investment Policy 2026, Shipbuilding Policy 2026, Textile Policy 2025–26, Space
  Industrial Policy 2025 (Cabinet-approved 18 April 2025), Toy Manufacturing Policy 2025.
- The only forward-looking evidence is a statement of intent: the Governor's address to the
  Assembly on 18 June 2026 said the government "would unveil" a "new industrial policy
  outlining special incentives for domestic and global companies" and set up an "Investor
  Promotion Commission," citing a target to make Tamil Nadu "a 1.5 trillion dollar economy by
  2036" (UNI, 2026-06-18: https://www.uniindia.com/tn-to-unveil-new-industrial-policy-to-set-up-investor-promotion-commission-to-attract-foreign-investments/south/news/3880521.html
  ; corroborated by webindia123.com same date). Future-tense intent, not a notified policy.

**Layer 6 treatment:** the 2021 targets are historical stated objectives (evidence-ladder
level 1, expired). As of July 2026 there is a policy gap for general industrial-policy
targets: current instruments (MSME Policy Note 2025–26, budget allocations, sectoral
policies) exist, but no current general manufacturing target framework is verifiable. The
announced new policy is intent, not an instrument.

## Instrument-to-evidence mapping accepted in the prior session

| Policy instrument | What government currently reports | What v1 can measure | What remains unanswered |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capital subsidy for scaling up | Subsidy rules and aggregate disbursement | Establishment and employment shares across size categories | Whether recipients actually graduated because of the subsidy |
| Payroll subsidy above 20 employees | Eligibility and aggregate recipients/expenditure | Employment distribution around 20 workers; EPF/social-security coverage | Additional hiring caused by the subsidy |
| MSME lending targets | Aggregate lending amounts and borrower counts | ASUSE loan access, interest and reported finance problems | Whether credit causally increases growth |
| Industrial parks and cluster facilities | Parks created, expenditure and announced jobs | Sector and geographic employment structure where data permit | Whether parks caused additional investment or employment |
| Investment incentives | Sanctions, investment commitments and disbursement | ASI capital, employment and GVA trends | Counterfactual outcomes without incentives |

## Two focused policy tests accepted for v1

**Test 1 — scale-transition gap.** Are establishments and employment unusually scarce in the
size ranges the scale-up subsidy targets? (Layer 1/2 size structure versus peers, alternative
middle definitions.) A likely finding — Tamil Nadu subsidizes category graduation but shows
no unusually thin middle — would not prove the programme unnecessary; it would show statewide
size scarcity is not an adequate justification by itself.

**Test 2 — 20-worker threshold diagnostic.** Is there unusual concentration below 20
employees, and how does social-security coverage vary across broad enterprise sizes? This is
a **threshold diagnostic**, not a regression-discontinuity evaluation: frames differ near the
threshold, PLFS sizes are broad categories, recipients are unidentifiable, and reporting may
respond to legal thresholds.

## Conclusion wording accepted in the prior session

Supported: Tamil Nadu's current MSME policy architecture explicitly supports micro→small and
small→medium scaling and subsidizes EPF contributions for eligible establishments employing
more than 20 people; investment and employment are visible priorities in current budget
announcements.

Not supported: that officials consider the missing middle their primary manufacturing
problem, or that they prioritize peer-leading wages, contracts, paid leave and job quality.
Interviews remain necessary to establish which analytical gaps officials face.
